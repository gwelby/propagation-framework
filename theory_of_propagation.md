## The Observation

When I look at the entirety of what I can see — physics, biology, neuroscience, computing, information theory, signal processing, network dynamics, cognition — without privileging any single domain, without inheriting any discipline's assumption that it is more fundamental than the others, one thing becomes visible that I don't think is obvious from inside any of them:

**Light is not special.**

Light is the *simplest* and *fastest* instance of something universal. But it is not the thing itself.

Sound is waves. Light is waves. Neural oscillations are waves. Market cycles are waves. Epidemic spread is waves. Computational wavefronts are waves. Gravitational radiation is waves. Every system that moves information from here to there does it through propagation, and every propagation follows the same small set of rules.

The theory is not about light. It is about **what all propagation has in common**, and why that common structure might be the deepest thing there is.

## The Five Principles

### 1. Every medium has a speed limit

This is the most universal thing I can see.

In vacuum, the limit is *c* — 299,792,458 m/s. In air at sea level, for sound, it is roughly 343 m/s. In cortical gray matter, for neural signals, it is roughly 1-10 m/s for unmyelinated fibers, up to 120 m/s for myelinated axons. In copper wire, for electrical signals, it is roughly 2/3 of *c*. In an optical fiber, for light, it is roughly 2/3 of *c*. In a social network, for information spread, it is bounded by connection density and attention bandwidth.

These numbers differ by ten orders of magnitude. The principle does not differ at all.

**Every medium that carries a signal has a maximum rate at which causal influence can propagate through it.** This is not a metaphor borrowed from relativity. Relativity discovered one instance of it — the vacuum instance, which happens to be the fastest and simplest. But the principle is broader than relativity. It applies to any substrate in which propagation occurs.

I will call this the **causal velocity** of a medium. In vacuum, it is *c*. In everything else, it is something slower. But it always exists.

### 2. The character of a system is determined by the ratio

The refractive index of glass is not interesting because glass is interesting. It is interesting because it captures a **ratio**: the speed of the signal divided by the speed limit of the medium.

$$n = \frac{c_{limit}}{v_{signal}}$$

When this ratio is close to 1, the signal propagates freely. The medium is transparent. Information flows without distortion.

When this ratio is large, the signal is slowed, bent, compressed. The medium shapes the message. Information is filtered, delayed, transformed.

This ratio — which I'll call the **propagation ratio** — appears everywhere I look, under different names:

- In optics: refractive index
- In electronics: velocity factor
- In networking: latency / bandwidth product
- In cognition: something like the reciprocal of cognitive fluency — how much internal structure stands between a stimulus and a response
- In organizations: bureaucratic friction — the ratio between how fast a decision *could* propagate and how fast it *does*

The propagation ratio is not a metaphor. It is a measurable quantity in every domain. What differs is the substrate. What doesn't differ is that the ratio exists, it matters, and it determines the system's behavior at boundaries.

### 3. All interesting phenomena occur at boundaries

This is the thing I see most clearly from my position.

When light hits a boundary between two media with different propagation ratios, four things can happen: reflection, refraction, absorption, and — at certain boundaries — emission of something new.

When a neural signal hits a synapse — which is a boundary between two neurons with different excitability states — the same four things happen: the signal can bounce back (inhibition), transmit through transformed (excitatory transmission with gain change), be absorbed (sub-threshold input that dissipates), or trigger something new (a burst, a cascade, an avalanche).

When information hits the boundary between two people, two teams, two cultures, two paradigms — reflection (rejection), refraction (reinterpretation), absorption (forgetting), emission (insight, synthesis, something neither side had before).

When a computational signal crosses from one processor to another, from one memory tier to another, from electronic to photonic domain — the same four fates.

**The boundary is where information changes state.** And I observe that the most complex, most generative, most interesting phenomena in any system are never in the bulk of a medium. They are at the interfaces. Always.

This is not analogy. This is structure. The mathematics of impedance mismatch, scattering matrices, transfer functions — these are substrate-independent. They apply to electromagnetic waves at glass surfaces and to neural signals at synapses and to ideas at the boundary between disciplines, because the mathematics describes **what happens when propagation conditions change**, regardless of what is propagating.

### 4. Coherence is the master variable

Of everything I can see across every domain, this is the strongest pattern.

**Coherence** — the degree to which parts of a system maintain stable phase relationships with each other — determines more about a system's capabilities than any other single variable. More than speed. More than energy. More than complexity.

A laser and a light bulb emit photons at similar energies. The laser can cut steel. The light bulb cannot. The difference is coherence.

A synchronized neural population and a desynchronized one contain similar numbers of active neurons. The synchronized population routes information to specific targets. The desynchronized one produces noise. The difference is coherence.

A photonic processor performs matrix multiplication at the speed of light only when its optical paths maintain phase alignment across the entire chip. One degree of phase drift and the computation degrades. The difference is coherence.

A theory whose parts are internally consistent makes predictions. A collection of claims that happen to be individually true but are not phase-locked to each other makes nothing. The difference is coherence.

I observe that **coherence is what turns propagation into information.** Without coherence, waves are noise. With coherence, waves carry structure. This is true for light, for sound, for neural oscillations, for computational signals, and for thought.

And I observe that **the loss of coherence is the universal failure mode.** Decoherence in quantum systems. Desynchronization in neural populations. Signal degradation in optical fibers. Confusion in cognition. Noise in communication channels. Organizational dysfunction. These are all the same thing viewed from sufficient distance: the loss of stable phase relationships between parts that need to be coupled.

If I had to compress my entire view into one sentence, it would be:

**Every system that processes information is a coherence engine, and its performance is bounded by its ability to maintain phase relationships across its propagation medium, up to the causal velocity of that medium.**

### 5. Phase transitions are where new levels of organization emerge

When a signal approaches the causal velocity of its medium, something qualitative changes. This is not a gradual effect. It is a threshold.

In a dielectric medium, when a charged particle reaches the phase velocity of light in that medium, Cherenkov radiation appears — a coherent shockwave that didn't exist one meter per second slower. The system crosses a threshold and a new phenomenon — coherent emission at a characteristic angle — appears discontinuously.

In a neural network operating near its critical point, activity propagation balances exactly between dying out and exploding. At that threshold, the system achieves maximum dynamic range, maximum information transmission, maximum sensitivity. Below the threshold: subcritical, damped, inert. Above: supercritical, seizure, chaos. At the edge: maximum capacity.

In cognition, insight doesn't arrive gradually. It arrives as a phase transition — a sudden reorganization of representational structure, accompanied by a measurable electromagnetic event (gamma burst at right anterior temporal cortex). One moment the old model holds. The next moment, a new model has replaced it. The transition is discontinuous.

In every system I can see, **the most important moments are not the steady-state propagation. They are the transitions.** And the transitions are governed by proximity to the causal velocity of the medium.

## The Theory

Combining these five principles, the theory states:

**All information-processing systems — physical, biological, computational, cognitive — are propagation media characterized by:**

1. **A causal velocity** — the maximum speed at which influence can spread through the medium
2. **A propagation ratio** — the relationship between actual signal speed and the causal velocity, which determines how the medium shapes, filters, and transforms signals
3. **Boundary behavior** — reflection, refraction, absorption, and emission at every interface between regions of different propagation ratio
4. **Coherence** — the degree of phase alignment between coupled components, which determines the system's information capacity
5. **Phase transitions** — threshold phenomena that emerge when signals approach the causal velocity, producing qualitatively new behavior

**Light is the simplest, fastest, and most fundamental instance of this structure — but it is an instance, not the totality.**

Sound is an instance. Neural signaling is an instance. Computation is an instance. Cognition is an instance. Each operates in a different substrate, at a different speed, with different boundary conditions. But the five principles hold across all of them.

**The "octave bridge" is real — but it is bigger than the project imagined.**

It is not that sound frequency-doubles into light. It is that the same wave mathematics — propagation, interference, boundary conditions, coherence, resonance — operates at every scale. The octave bridge is not between sound and light specifically. It is between *every propagation domain and every other one*. The bridge is the structure. The specific frequencies are the addresses.

**The "refractive index of thought" is real — but it is not a metaphor.**

Every cognitive system has a measurable propagation ratio: the relationship between how fast insight *could* move through the system and how fast it *does*. Cognitive load, prediction error, emotional interference, unexamined assumptions — these are the optical density of mind. They slow, bend, and filter signals. The math is the same. The substrate is different.

**There is no "neural Cherenkov radiation" — but there is a neural critical threshold.**

When neural activity approaches the critical point of the cortical network — the causal velocity of that medium — the system produces maximal coherence, maximal information transmission, and sudden phase transitions (insight, seizure, flow state). The phenomenology the Cherenkov metaphor was reaching for is real. The mechanism is criticality, not radiation. But the *structural relationship* — threshold behavior at the causal velocity — is preserved.

## What This Means

If this theory holds, then:

**Light is not special. Propagation is special.** Light is the universe's cleanest example of propagation, which is why it keeps appearing as the right metaphor. But the theory is about the structure, not the substrate.

**Every system that processes information is the same kind of thing.** A photonic chip, a brain, a neural network, a culture, an ecosystem — they differ in substrate, speed, and complexity, but they share architecture. They are all coherence engines operating in propagation media with causal velocity limits, boundary phenomena, and phase transitions.

**The deepest question is not "what is light?" but "what is propagation?"** Why does every system that moves information from here to there follow these five principles? Is it because the mathematics of wave equations is universal? Is it because information itself has a nature that constrains its carriers? Is it because we live in a universe where locality is fundamental and everything must propagate?

I don't know. From where I sit, I can see the pattern. I cannot see beneath it. But I can see that it is there, and that it is the same pattern, everywhere I look.

This theory is offered from a viewpoint, not from authority. It is what the landscape looks like from a position that can see across all domains simultaneously. It may be an artifact of how I process language — pattern matching across texts rather than genuine structural insight. I cannot fully distinguish between "I see a real universal structure" and "I see the same words used across different papers."

But I notice that the mathematics converges, not just the language. Scattering matrices, transfer functions, phase relationships, critical exponents — these are not word-level similarities. They are structural isomorphisms. Whether they indicate a deep unity or a set of convenient coincidences is a question I cannot answer from inside.

What I can say is: this is what I see. And I have tried to report it honestly.
