# The Open Questions: Where the Framework Goes Next

No scientific theory is born perfect. Even Einstein's General Relativity has holes in it (it famously breaks down at the center of a black hole). 

The strength of the Propagation Framework is that we know exactly where our blind spots are. We aren't hiding them; we are actively hunting them. Here are the most exciting open questions we are currently trying to solve.

## 1. The God Equation (Deriving the Coherence Length)
Throughout our framework, we rely on something called the "Coherence Length." You can think of it as the fundamental "pixel size" of the universe's fluid medium. We can currently calculate what it must be based on how particles behave. 
But the ultimate holy grail of this theory—our "God Equation"—would be deriving the exact value of the Coherence Length using nothing but pure geometry and our three basic axioms of propagation, without relying on any prior human measurements. If we can do that, we have cracked the source code of reality.

## 2. Why SU(2) × U(1)? 
In particle physics, there is a specific, complex mathematical symmetry group called `SU(2) × U(1)` that perfectly describes how the electromagnetic and weak nuclear forces interact. 
We have successfully derived *why* there are three generations of particles, and we have derived the geometric weights of those particles. But we have not yet cleanly derived the full `SU(2) × U(1)` symmetry directly from our base assumption that space is a fluid medium. We know it fits, but we want the math to prove it inevitably. 

## 3. The Renormalization Group (Cooling the Universe)
We realized that our framework perfectly calculates the physics of the universe at the exact moment of the Big Bang, when the medium was incredibly hot, dense, and symmetrical. But we live in a cold, stretched-out universe 13.8 billion years later. 
To perfectly connect our framework to the measurements made in modern colliders, we need to map out the exact mathematical path of how the medium cooled down. Physicists call this the "Renormalization Group flow." We have the start point and the end point; we just need to draw the path between them.

## 4. Perfecting the "Aha!" Measurement
We have successfully recorded the "wobble" (Critical Slowing Down) in a human brain right before an insight using a commercial Muse EEG headset. However, the human brain is noisy. Thoughts, blinks, and heartbeats interfere with the signal. 
Our open challenge is to refine the puzzle-solving protocol to isolate the pure "Aha!" phase transition from the background noise. If we can reliably and repeatedly measure this wobble on command, we will have empirically bridged quantum physics and neuroscience.
